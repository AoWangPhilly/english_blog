Beethoven’s Eroica and Supergirl’s Dreamer

The Hero’s Journey of Two Superheroes

Introduction

Beginning the 19th century sparked the transition of the Classical era to the Romantic era thanks to the rebel Ludwig van Beethoven. It broke through the Classical’s traditions in length and form. However, many of the Viennese society thought it bizarre, but as a rebel - he made them rethink what a symphony should be and do.

Much like another rebel, Nia Nal, or Dreamer from Supergirl, made a nation, much like the U.S., going through an anti-alien era, full of xenophobic radicalization, believe that both aliens and humans can live in peace. And much like Beethoven, she overcame her conflicts of self-doubt and embraced her powers to take up the mantle.

Before moving on, the hero’s journey shouldn’t be used as a template for all stories. Therefore we shouldn’t think Eroica and Dreamer should match it exactly, but as it’s a way to analyze these two great works and how they relate and differ from each other.

Status Quo

Nia Nal moved to National City to learn from Kara Danvers, Supergirl, to become a reporter. But even as a rising reporter and without even realizing she had powers, she stood up to anti-alien supporters. When Brainy was ordering pizzas from a shop owner he thought was his friend, his image inducer, the device that makes him look human, was hacked.

After Brainy was revealed, the pizza shop owner attempted to attack him with a steel baseball bat. Nia defended him, being reminded how Aliens and trans were discriminated against and stood up and held a mirror to their faces, showing they, themselves are traitors.

Call to Adventure

In the Call to Adventure, the quest often passes through an unfamiliar, special world, but always starts and ends in the ordinary one. She returns to her hometown, Parthas, Irish, for paradise, “where aliens and humans lived together in harmony for decades,” for the Harvest Fest.

Nia sees her family and sister against but tries to mask her dreaming powers from them, asking Kara to not even mentioning it to her family due to her sister eagerly waiting for her abilities to arrive. The powers are passed down matrilineally; only one daughter can receive the power. Her sister has devoted her life, assuming she would accept it.

Assistance

Unlike Dreamer's assistance, which was Supergirl and Brainy helping her to become a superhero, Beethoven didn't have that luxury. But I think it was more of the Heiligenstadt environment and his own love for music that was assistance. In it self, possibly one's own inner-strength can be accounted as assistance in the hero's journey.

Trials

Eroica’s Second Movement, titled “Funeral March” is fitting with the oboe solo, creating an ambience of grief and sadness. And it evokes these feelings, since it was years before he wrote Eroica that he was going deaf. He felt terror and shame, trying to keep it a secret. Thinking to himself, “What kind of musician cannot hear?”

I feel that this stage aligns with Nia’s storyline, since it’s just when her mother finds out that she inherited the Dreamer power and dies. With her sister stating it’s her fault that her mother died, since she should’ve gotten the Dreamer power already and could’ve saved her, Nia is conflicted with both grief and guilt from hiding that she has it and has been hiding it from her, though encouraging that she’ll get it ONE DAY. But imagine adding shame on top that, thinking if I were like my sister, studying how to interpret dreams and being serious about the powers, I could’ve saved my mother.
